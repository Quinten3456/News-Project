   McKinsey Insights | Tier 2 | Mar 30

   What happened: A McKinsey-Serviceware analysis of 17 global enterprises found that AI is absorbing up to one-third of technology change budgets while simultaneously inflating run costs, forcing CIOs to rethink the fundamental run/change spending split.
   Why it matters: Enterprises that fail to architect deliberate retirement of legacy systems risk cost accumulation rather than cost transformation, eroding the efficiency gains AI is supposed to deliver. The run/change imbalance also signals a structural capability risk: organizations locked into high run-cost bases will have less flexibility to fund the next wave of AI-driven operating model shifts.
   Strategic implication: Technology strategists should audit whether AI investments are driving net operating model simplification or merely layering new costs onto unreformed legacy stacks—and enforce hard decommissioning targets as a condition of AI budget approval.
   https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/mckinsey-technology/our-insights/recalibrating-technology-budgets-for-the-ai-era

PODCAST: ANTHROPIC'S RAPID FEATURE CADENCE RESHAPES AI COMPETITION
AI Report

  - Anthropic Claude's Accelerating Feature Releases: Anthropic launched multiple major Claude features within a single week, including Mac computer control (clicking, scrolling, opening apps, editing files) and a remote dispatch function allowing users to operate their home or office computer from a mobile device. The hosts framed this pace as potentially the new normal for frontier AI labs including OpenAI and Google. The velocity of capability deployment signals that competitive differentiation in AI is shifting from model quality to feature breadth and integration depth, requiring strategists to reassess product roadmap timelines.
  - Claude's Computer Use and Agentic Capabilities: Claude can now autonomously control a Mac computer as a human would, and the 'Dispatch' feature enables asynchronous task execution — for example, having the AI review emails and prepare a calendar before the user arrives at the office. These capabilities represent a move from conversational AI toward persistent, autonomous agents. Agentic AI that operates independently on user systems marks a critical inflection point for enterprise automation strategies and raises immediate questions around security, access governance, and workflow redesign.
  - User Migration from ChatGPT to Claude: The hosts noted a significant uptick in users asking how to switch from ChatGPT to Claude, partly driven by a public boycott movement around OpenAI in the Netherlands. Many users equate 'AI' with 'ChatGPT,' reflecting strong brand lock-in that Claude is now actively disrupting. Shifts in end-user allegiance at the consumer level can rapidly translate into enterprise procurement pressure, making it strategically important to monitor Claude's adoption trajectory as a signal of OpenAI's competitive moat eroding.
  - Anthropic Using Claude to Develop Itself: The hosts briefly noted that Anthropic is now using Claude internally to assist in developing new versions of the model, suggesting a recursive self-improvement loop is already in operation at the company. This was mentioned as a notable aside amid the broader feature announcements. AI-assisted AI development has profound implications for the pace of capability gains and raises governance questions about oversight, interpretability, and the compounding speed of frontier model advancement.

  This week's takeaway: Anthropic is executing a high-cadence, multi-front expansion — from agentic computer control to remote task dispatch — that is compressing the competitive response window for rivals and forcing a strategic rethink of what 'AI product leadership' means; technology strategists should treat weekly capability releases as the new baseline assumption, not the exception.
