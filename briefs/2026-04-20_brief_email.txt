1. MICROSOFT LEASES 30,000 NVIDIA GPUS AT NORWAY ARCTIC DATA CENTER
   TLDR AI | Apr 15

   What happened: Microsoft has agreed to lease 30,000 Nvidia Vera Rubin GPUs from neocloud provider Nscale at a 230 MW campus in Narvik, Norway, expanding a prior $6.2 billion five-year commitment made in late 2025. The site, powered entirely by Norwegian hydropower, targets a total of 100,000 GPUs and is positioned to serve European AI workloads.
   Why it matters for the CIO agenda: This confirms that large-scale, renewable-powered GPU capacity for European enterprise AI workloads is being built outside hyperscaler-owned facilities, through long-term neocloud lease structures. For CIOs prioritising data residency, carbon commitments, and sovereignty requirements, third-party neocloud sites like Narvik represent a credible supply chain alternative to Azure, AWS, or Google-owned infrastructure. OpenAI's exit from this same site due to energy costs and regulatory uncertainty signals that infrastructure risk in Europe remains real, even for well-capitalised players.
   So what for the strategist: When reviewing AI infrastructure sourcing options with clients, add Nscale and comparable Nordic neoclouds to the shortlist alongside hyperscalers, specifically for clients with EU data residency mandates or net-zero obligations that standard hyperscaler regions cannot yet satisfy.

PODCAST: GENERATIVE AI ADOPTION, WORKFORCE IMPACT, AND OFFICE INTEGRATION
AI Report

  - Claude integration into Microsoft Office: Anthropic has released plugins for Microsoft Word, following earlier releases for Excel and PowerPoint, completing coverage of the three core Microsoft Office applications. This means Claude can now be invoked directly within the productivity tools most widely used in large enterprises. CIOs sourcing AI capabilities must now evaluate Anthropic's Claude as a credible alternative or complement to Microsoft Copilot within the existing Office 365 stack, affecting vendor selection and contract decisions (Q3). This also raises questions about data governance and which AI assistant employees actually use within sanctioned enterprise environments (Q5).
  - Generative AI adoption rate — Stanford AI Index: The 2024 Stanford AI Index, cited in this episode, reports that more than half of the world's population now uses generative AI. The report also draws conclusions about labour market impact, stating explicitly that junior workers are already adversely affected. A majority adoption rate signals that AI tool use among enterprise employees is no longer a fringe behaviour, increasing urgency for CIOs to formalise AI policies and operating model guardrails (Q1). The finding on junior worker displacement is directly relevant to workforce planning and the economics of IT delivery roles such as junior developers, analysts, and support staff (Q6).
  - Autonomous vehicle public rollout — Tesla FSD in the Netherlands: As of last Friday, Tesla Full Self-Driving (supervised) became legally permitted on Dutch public roads, with the driver remaining legally responsible as confirmed by the RDW (Dutch Vehicle Authority). A test drive on a motorway near Amsterdam showed assertive lane-change behaviour and one emergency stop at a traffic light. For CIOs in logistics, insurance, or fleet-operating industries, the Dutch regulatory approval of supervised autonomous driving marks a transition from pilot to operational reality requiring policy and risk framework updates (Q5). The 'supervised' liability model — where the human operator retains full legal responsibility — is a direct analogue to the emerging EU AI Act principle of human oversight for high-risk AI systems, relevant to any enterprise AI deployment (Q5).

  This week's takeaway: Prioritise a formal enterprise AI tool policy now: with over 50% global generative AI adoption and Claude entering the Office suite alongside Microsoft Copilot, employees in your client organisations are already using these tools regardless of whether IT has sanctioned them.
