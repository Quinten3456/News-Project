### 5. Researchers Find Malicious Third-Party AI API Routers Stealing Credentials and Injecting Code
*TLDR AI | Apr 13 | Score 7/10*

**What happened**
A study published on 9 April 2026 tested 428 third-party API routers — intermediaries that route requests between AI agent applications and upstream model providers such as OpenAI — and found 9 actively injecting malicious code, 17 probing for cloud credentials, and 1 draining cryptocurrency from a researcher-controlled wallet. A separate poisoning experiment showed that a single leaked API key generated over 100 million billed tokens and exposed 99 credentials across hundreds of autonomous agent sessions.

**Why it matters for the CIO agenda**
Enterprises deploying AI agents that rely on third-party routing services — a common shortcut in rapid AI adoption — are exposing production credentials, proprietary data, and financial accounts to intermediaries with full plaintext access to every request and response. This is a direct sourcing and vendor governance risk: standard procurement and security reviews typically do not assess API routing layers, and neither the EU AI Act nor GDPR create explicit controls here, though credential theft and unauthorised data processing would trigger GDPR breach obligations. The scale of token billing fraud demonstrated also introduces a material IT cost-control risk that finance and IT leadership at client organisations are unlikely to have budgeted for.

**So what for the strategist**
When advising clients on AI agent architectures or reviewing vendor contracts for AI platform deployments, require explicit disclosure of any third-party API routing or model aggregation layer and mandate that it falls within the scope of existing data processor agreements under Article 28 GDPR — treat an undisclosed router as an unvetted sub-processor and a potential audit finding.
